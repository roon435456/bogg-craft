Cast Iron Coq au Vin

⏱ Prep: 40 min 🔥 Cook: 1 hr 45 min 🪵 Serves: 4–6 ⭐ Intermediate

Few dishes feel more at home in a cast iron pot than a properly made coq au vin. The heavy pan holds heat evenly — it does not rush, it does not panic — and that steady, unhurried warmth is exactly what this dish requires. The smell of it filling a cold cabin is, frankly, half the reason to make it at all.

The chicken must be of good quality. This is not a suggestion. A bird that has lived a full and vigorous life rewards you with flavor that no amount of wine can fake. If you sourced it well, you are already halfway there. If not, we will say nothing more about it.

What You'll Need

  • 1 whole chicken, cut into 8 pieces (~1.8kg)
  • 750ml full-bodied red wine — Burgundy or Rhône
  • 200g smoked lardons or thick-cut bacon
  • 250g pearl onions, peeled
  • 300g cremini mushrooms, halved
  • 4 cloves garlic, crushed
  • 2 sprigs fresh thyme
  • 2 bay leaves, 1 sprig rosemary
  • 3 tbsp tomato paste
  • 500ml good chicken stock
  • 3 tbsp all-purpose flour
  • 2 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 3 tbsp neutral oil
  • Salt and coarse black pepper
  • Fresh parsley, chopped, to finish

How to Make It

  1. Season chicken generously on all sides with salt and pepper. Do not be timid. Under-seasoned food is one of the great avoidable sorrows.
  2. In your cast iron over medium-high heat, render lardons until golden and crisp. Remove and set aside, leaving fat in the pan.
  3. Working in batches — never crowd the pan, it causes steaming instead of browning — sear chicken pieces 4 minutes per side until deeply golden.
  4. Remove chicken. Add butter, sauté onions and mushrooms until golden. Add garlic, cook one minute more.
  5. Dust everything with flour, stir to coat. Add tomato paste, cook 2 minutes stirring constantly.
  6. Return chicken and lardons. Pour over wine and stock. Add herbs. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a slow, comfortable simmer.
  7. Cover and cook 1 hour 15 minutes, turning chicken once at the halfway point.
  8. Adjust seasoning, remove herb sprigs. Serve in warmed bowls with crusty bread or mashed potatoes.
🔥 Fireside Note Cook with wine you would pour into a glass and drink. The old advice about cheap cooking wine is a myth told by people who haven't tasted the difference. A reliable village Burgundy is all you need — nothing grand, nothing forgettable.

Techniques Every Cabin Cook Must Know

The following techniques form the bedrock of any serious cook's knowledge. Master these and you can approach nearly any recipe with confidence — and a certain quiet assurance that is, in its own way, deeply satisfying around a fire.

🔪

Brunoise

Cutting vegetables into perfect 2mm cubes. Requires a sharp knife, patience, and many practice carrots before achieving any elegance.

🍳

Sauté

High heat, minimal fat, constant motion. The pan must be hot before the food enters. This is critical. A cold pan is an insult to the ingredient.

🌡️

Sous Vide

Precision cooking in a temperature-controlled bath. Yields remarkably consistent results. Popular among those who have exchanged fire for science.

🥄

Emulsify

Binding fat and water into a stable sauce. Hollandaise, mayonnaise, vinaigrette — all emulsions, all worth learning inside and out.

🫕

Braising

Long, slow cooking in a small amount of liquid. Transforms the toughest cuts into the most tender results. Time is the primary ingredient here.

🔥

Flambé

Add alcohol, ignite it. Burns off raw spirit flavor, caramelizes sugars. Also impresses whoever is watching from across the cabin considerably.

Skillet Tarte Tatin

⏱ Prep: 25 min 🔥 Cook: 45 min 🪵 Serves: 6–8 ⭐ Moderate

The tarte Tatin was, famously, an accident — or so the story goes. The Tatin sisters, proprietors of a hotel in the Loire Valley, supposedly invented it by mistake sometime in the 1880s, though food historians debate the precise details with great energy and no visible sign of resolution.

What is not debatable is the result: caramelized apples, sticky and soft, atop a buttery pastry crust, presented upside-down so that the dark caramel runs down the sides. This is the sort of dessert that makes people go quiet at the table. The good kind of quiet.

What You'll Need

  • 1.2kg firm apples (Granny Smith or Braeburn)
  • 150g caster sugar
  • 80g unsalted butter, cubed
  • 1 vanilla pod, seeds scraped
  • Pinch of fleur de sel
  • ½ tsp ground cinnamon
  • 1 sheet all-butter puff pastry (320g)
  • Crème fraîche or vanilla ice cream to serve

How to Make It

  1. Peel, core, quarter apples. Toss with half the sugar, rest 20 minutes, drain well. Excess moisture is the enemy of good caramel.
  2. In a 24cm ovenproof skillet, melt butter over medium heat. Add remaining sugar; cook without stirring until deep amber, about 10–12 minutes.
  3. Off heat. Add vanilla and salt. Pack apple quarters tightly in concentric circles — they shrink during cooking, so crowd them in.
  4. Return to medium heat, cook apples 15 minutes until softened. Remove and cool slightly.
  5. Preheat oven to 200°C. Drape pastry over apples, tuck edges around the fruit.
  6. Bake 25–30 minutes until pastry is deep golden. Cool 10 minutes — not longer, or caramel sets — then invert onto a plate with confidence and commitment.
🔥 On the Subject of Caramel Caramel demands your full attention. Do not stir it, do not check your messages, do not hold a conversation while it cooks. The window between perfect amber and a substance that requires you to throw away the pan is measured in seconds. We have all been there. It does not get easier. It just gets faster to notice.

The Philosophy of Salt

Of all the ingredients in any cook's pantry, salt is the most democratic, the most transformative, and the most frequently misunderstood. Salt does not merely make food taste salty. This is a common misconception that has led well-intentioned home cooks to under-season their food for decades, resulting in flat, apologetic meals served to people too polite to say a word.

What salt actually does is considerably more interesting. It suppresses bitterness, amplifies aromatics, enhances sweetness, and draws out the deep savory quality that makes food feel satisfying rather than merely edible. A chocolate cake without salt tastes one-dimensional. A tomato sauce without enough salt tastes raw and somehow unfinished, as though the cook left the room and forgot to come back.

A Note on Pasta Water

Your pasta water should taste like the sea. This instruction — repeated in cookbooks until it has become a cliché — is nonetheless correct. A large pot of water requires two to three tablespoons of salt to be properly seasoned. This sounds like too much. It is not. Cooks who have spent years under-salting pasta water often describe the moment they eat properly seasoned pasta in a good Italian kitchen as a small awakening. You can have that awakening at home. Tonight, even.

The Four Salts Worth Knowing

Fine sea salt for everyday cooking and pasta water. Kosher salt for brining and dry rubs, where its large crystals allow precision. Fleur de sel or Maldon flakes for finishing — scattered over a steak or a ripe tomato at the last moment. Smoked salt, used sparingly, for those times you want depth without lighting an actual fire. Though if you are reading this guide, you probably have access to an actual fire, and that is better still.

A Glossary of Culinary Terms

The professional kitchen speaks in a specialized language. The following definitions provide a working vocabulary sufficient for understanding most recipes — and, if deployed in conversation, conveying a convincing impression of expertise.

Mise en Place "Everything in its place." All ingredients measured and prepped before cooking begins. The foundation of the professional kitchen and a philosophy applicable, one suspects, to many other things.
Deglaze Adding liquid to a hot pan after searing in order to dissolve the caramelized fond. The resulting liquid is the beginning of almost every great pan sauce in the history of cooking.
Julienne Long, thin matchstick cuts about 3mm square. Used for vegetables in stir-fries, salads, and garnishes. Requires more patience than most people expect the first time.
Fond The browned bits left in a pan after searing. Not debris — concentrated flavor. Treat them with the reverence they deserve. Scrape them up. Build something from them.
Blanch & Shock Brief boiling followed by ice water to halt cooking instantly. Preserves color, texture, nutrients. A simple technique that makes vegetables look significantly more intentional.
Beurre Blanc A French butter sauce: reduced white wine and shallots, finished with cold butter whisked in piece by piece. Difficult the first time. Impressive every time after that.
Chiffonade Stacking, rolling, and thinly slicing leafy herbs into ribbons. Used for basil and mint primarily. A technical name for something most people do without knowing it has a name.
Resting Allowing cooked meat to sit before cutting so that juices redistribute. Cutting too soon results in those juices running onto the board instead of staying in the meat. A small patience with a large reward.

The 5 Mother Sauces

Béchamel — Butter, flour, milk. Foundation of gratins and every great mac and cheese.

Velouté — Butter, flour, light stock. Elegant and endlessly adaptable.

Espagnole — Brown stock, mirepoix, tomato. The darkest and richest of the five.

Sauce Tomat — Tomatoes cooked low and slow with aromatics until they surrender.

Hollandaise — Egg yolks and butter. Temperamental, rewarding, worth every attempt.
